Wildlife Farming: Industry Profile

In the early history of South Africa, hunting played an important role in subsistence and rural economics. By 1964, hunting pressure and agricultural expansion had reduced wildlife numbers to critically low levels. However, in the last 50 years, regulated hunting and its associated activities have created a demand for venison, wildlife products, and eco-tourism.
Since the ratification of the Game Theft Act in 1991, which granted private entities the right to own and trade wildlife in South Africa, wildlife populations have increased dramatically. Today, an estimated 16 million Heads of game are privately owned in South Africa, approximately 4x more than in formal State owned conservation areas (Absa, 2015).

The wildlife farming industry in South Africa represents some R10 Billion per annum in revenue, approximately 5% of the agricultural GDP of South Africa (Dept. Environmental Affairs, 2015), and is estimated to employ over 100 000 workers (Els, 2012). The 2013 Absa Agriculture Outlook shows that the industry encompasses more than 9000 private wildlife ranches and utilizes more than 16.8% of the country’s landmass (Absa, 2013).

Over the period 2008-2015, the total wildlife industry market grew by more than 14% per year; with an annual growth exceeding 6% in domestic hunting, a decrease in international hunting, and unprecedented growth in live auction sales (Dept. Environmental Affairs, 2015).

"The game farming industry in South Africa has progressed to, not only become a world leader in extensive sustainable utilization of game species, but has now tapped into the local and international meat market."

~ Agriculture, Forestry, and Fisheries., Dept of (DAFF, 2012)

Game Farming Value Chain

The Wildlife farming value chain is centered on activities that relate to the stocking, trading, breeding, and hunting of game, and all the services and goods required to support this value chain. The key drivers (in order of decreasing size) are domestic hunters, game trading and supporting services, international hunters, and a growing retail market demand for wildlife products such as venison, exotic leather, and taxidermy products. This industry is therefore an interesting combination of agriculture, ecotourism and conservation.

South Africa is the 20th largest venison producer in the world, producing an estimated 18 000 tons of Game meat in 2009. The top venison producers are Papua New Guinea and the United States, with an estimated combined production of 565 000 tons (Dept. Agriculture, Forestry, and Fisheries., 2009). The New Zealand Deer Industry highlights the potential of the South African venison market to bring international money into the country. In 2015 the N.Z. Deer industry exported R2.54 billion of venison and R674 million of co-products (Deer Industry NZ, 2016).
