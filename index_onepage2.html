Wildlife Farming: Industry Profile

  • Inception of formal industry: 1991, with the ratification of the Game theft act, which granted private entities the right to own and trade wildlife in South Africa
  • Annual revenue: R10 Billion, 5% of the agricultural GDP of South Africa (Dept. Environmental Affairs, 2015)
  • Land: More than 9000 privately owned wildlife ranches, covering 16.8% of the country’s landmass (Absa, 2013)
  • Workforce: Over 100 000 employed (Els, 2012)
  • Animals: An estimated 20 million heads of game, 80% of which are privately owned (Dept. Environmental Affairs, 2015)
  • Growth: Over the period 2008-2015, the total wildlife industry market grew by more than 14% per year (Dept. Environmental Affairs, 2015)

The premise behind game ranching is that game ranches can operate with lower input and management costs because native animals are better adapted to local environmental conditions, including heat and drought, and have greater resistance to diseases, parasites and toxic plants Habitat degradation is a problem throughout many African grazing lands, but game animals can be kept at high stocking density because the mix of browsers and grazers makes optimal use of all vegetation types

In South Africa, over 10,000 wildlife ranches have transformed 25 million ha of marginal agricultural land with limited water availability into thriving land use operations with far greater economic output than from traditional livestock enterprises (Dry, 2012). A typical commercial game ranch generates an economic output of about $31/ha compared to $11/ha for conventional livestock farming and creates better paid jobs for a more skilled workforce. According to Dry (2011) game ranching is the fifth largest contributor to the agricultural sector.

"The game farming industry in South Africa has progressed to, not only become a world leader in extensive sustainable utilization of game species, but has now tapped into the local and international meat market." ~ Agriculture, Forestry, and Fisheries., Dept of (Dept. Agriculture, Forestry, and Fisheries., 2009)

Game Farming Value Chain

The Wildlife farming value chain is centered on activities that relate to the stocking, trading, breeding, and hunting of game, and all the services and goods required to support this value chain. The key drivers (in order of decreasing size) are domestic hunters, game trading and supporting services, international hunters, and a growing retail market demand for wildlife products such as venison, exotic leather, and taxidermy products. This industry is therefore an interesting combination of agriculture, ecotourism and conservation.

Growing demand for venison in Africa

No continent will be struck as severely by the impacts of climate change as Africa. By 2020, it is predicted that water stress will reduce yields from rain-fed agriculture by up to 50% (IPCC, 2007).
It is likely that venison, which has a significantly lower water/kg requirement, will become an increasingly attractive alternative to beef and pork; particularly amongst poorer demographics.

Currently, South Africa is the 20th largest venison producer in the world, producing an estimated 18 000 tons of Game meat in 2009. The top venison producers are Papua New Guinea and the United States, with an estimated combined production of 565 000 tons (Dept. Agriculture, Forestry, and Fisheries., 2009).

Nagoya Protocol and species trade restrictions

South Africa is party to the following conventions and protocols that safeguard against the over-exploitation of animals:
  • Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), Having three core principles:
    • The conservation of Biological Diversity
    • Ensuring the sustainable use of biological resources, and
    • Promoting fair and equitable sharing of benefits arising from National genetic resources.
  • Convention on International Trade of Endangered Species (CITES), 1971:
    An international conservation cooperation and trade treaty which regulates and safeguards species from over-exploitation that threatens their survival.
    For more information: cites.org
  • Nagoya protocol, 2010:
    Relating to the access to genetic resources and the fair and equitable sharing of benefits arising from their utilization
    For more information: Nagoya Protocol, UN

Hunting in South Africa

South Africa has a rich history of hunting across all race groups. In the early history of South Africa, hunting played a crucial role in subsistence (meat) and for wildlife products (skins). By 1964, hunting pressures had reduced wildlife numbers to very low levels. However, in the last 50 years, regulated hunting and its associated activities have created a demand for venison, wildlife products, and eco-tourism. As a result, wildlife populations have grown dramatically, and today South Africa is home to estimated 20 million wild animals (16 million of these animals are privately owned, with only 4 million owned by the state) (ABSA, 2015)

Risk Protection

Risk Protection

  • The South African wildlife farming industry is a mature and robust market built upon a multi-billion Rand value-chain.Just as with any investment, investing in wildlife carries its own risks.
  • Inclusive in our offering is animal insurance that reduces exposure to the risks of animal loss through predation, disease, and natural disasters.
  • In addition, the farm insures against all losses that may occur due to a failure of its infrastructure or avoidable negligence. (fences,feeding etc.)

Risks

Drought:
  • All partner farms are required to have reliable access to water, supplemental feed, and emergency capital
  • Indigenous wildlife is well adapted to survive in the harsh local environment
Disease:
  • Disease surveillance and management is required by all our partner farms
Poaching & Predators:
  • Poaching is not a significant concern for most species, and predators are kept at bay with predator-proof fencing
Market Decline:
  • Though the value of an animal on the local game market may undulate, the exponential nature of breeding significantly mitigates the investor’s exposure to risk
  • For example, if the market value of your animals drop by 50%, you will still make profit after 5 years of breeding, as your breeding herd can be up to 400% larger
  • The value of the animals rest securely on a multi-billion rand value chain.

Profit

Profits are attained through:

  • The sale of the entire herd at the termination of the 5 year program
  • The selling of shares in the herd, to other investors
  • Annually: Low-value offspring will be sold annually to pay for the operational costs associated with the herd. The capital remaining will be split between the investor and WILDinvest, according to the profit-share agreement

Profit-share agreement:

  • The investor retains 100% ownership over the animals purchased at the start of the agreement; and can sell these animals at any stage, after they have given birth to the first generation of offspring. All profits from the sale of the starting herd go to the investor
  • Ownership of the offspring is shared evenly between the investor and WILDinvest/farm
